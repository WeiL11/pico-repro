💡 What I find most interesting

Reading across her papers, the question Alisa Liu keeps returning to is: the parts of the language-model pipeline everyone treats as fixed plumbing — the training data, the decoding step, and above all the tokenizer — are exactly where the real, under-exploited leverage lives.

Two moves recur. First, change what a model does without retraining it: steer it at decode time (DExperts → proxy-tuning, the same logit-arithmetic idea scaled up over years). Second, question the inputs we take for granted: is the data good (WANLI)? is the tokenizer — a step almost nobody examines — good, and what does it secretly leak (Data Mixture Inference, SuperBPE)? If I had to name what she’s really drawn to: overlooked, high-leverage layers and where models silently fail (ambiguity, hallucination) — finding a small, cheap lever that quietly moves a big model rather than spending more compute.

DExperts: Decoding-Time Controlled Text Generation with Experts & Anti-Experts 1st author

Steer a frozen LM toward/away from an attribute by adding a small expert−anti-expert logit difference at decode time — no retraining.

Intro — why

Large LMs can produce toxic/off-tone text, and retraining a 175B+ model to fix it is “computationally infeasible for most researchers” — and impossible for API-only models. We need cheap, decode-time control that needs only the model’s output probabilities and can steer both toward a good attribute and away from a bad one.

Method — how

Keep a frozen base M; add a small expert M⁺ (trained on text with the wanted attribute) and anti-expert M⁻ (the unwanted attribute). Combine their logits as a product of experts (multiplying distributions = a logical AND):

softmax( z + α (z+ − z−) )

The difference (z⁺−z⁻) is where the attribute signal lives; α is the strength knob. They first truncate to the base model’s top-k/top-p tokens so the experts can only re-rank plausible words (keeping fluency). An “anti-only” variant needs only negative examples.

Results

Detoxification (GPT-2 Large; RealToxicityPrompts; Perspective API): toxicity probability 0.520 → 0.128 with fluency near the base (ppl ~32 vs GeDi’s 60), beating DAPT/GeDi/PPLM. Very data-efficient: ~650 toxic comments and ~3 min of fine-tuning already match the strongest baseline. It also steers GPT-3 via the API (top-100 log-probs): toxicity prob 0.515 → 0.111. Sentiment control too (94.5% positive on neutral prompts).

Discussion & future

Negative steering is easier than positive; domain-narrow experts (movie reviews) trade fluency for control; stylistic rewriting needs more work. Future: personalised experts and better definitions of harm.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the method is only as good — and as fair — as the data its experts learn from and the metric judging it. The headline detox numbers use Perspective API, which the authors note over-flags minority-identity language, so “less toxic” can mean “scrubbed of minority-associated language.” Always validate with human judgement on your own domain (as the paper’s own MTurk study does).

Alisa Liu (1st), M. Sap, X. Lu, S. Swayamdipta, C. Bhagavatula, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. ACL 2021. ACL · arXiv · code

WANLI: Worker & AI Collaboration for NLI Dataset Creation 1st author

A 107,885-example NLI dataset where GPT-3 drafts examples in the hard, ambiguous style of MultiNLI and humans label + lightly revise — 4× smaller than MultiNLI yet trains more robust models.

Intro — why

NLI: given a premise and hypothesis, decide entailment / contradiction / neutral. Crowdsourced NLI is full of annotation artifacts (a model can guess the label from the hypothesis alone), so models look great in-domain but are brittle out-of-distribution. WANLI flips the roles: the LM generates, humans evaluate & correct.

Method — how

Four stages. (1) Dataset cartography tracks a model’s predictions across training epochs to find ambiguous (high-variability) examples, which build robustness; seed from the top-25% most ambiguous MultiNLI. (2) Take each seed’s 4 nearest same-label neighbours (via [CLS] embeddings) to capture a reasoning pattern, and ask GPT-3 to write a new example in that pattern. (3) Filter with an “estimated max variability” metric, keeping examples that look ambiguous to the model. (4) Two crowdworkers per example assign the gold label and make minimal edits (heavy revision re-introduces artifacts).

Results

107,885 examples (~4× smaller than MultiNLI’s 393k). Training on WANLI instead of MultiNLI improves all 8 out-of-distribution test sets: +4% Diagnostics, +11% HANS, +9% Adversarial NLI — and beats baselines with up to 9× more data. Fewer artifacts: a hypothesis-only model scores only 41.6% on WANLI vs 49.6% on MultiNLI. (κ = 0.60 worker agreement; only 3.54% of examples revised.)

Discussion & future

English-NLI only; genuinely ambiguous cases are forced into one label; GPT-3 reproduces contradiction poorly (~15%). Future: other languages/tasks; treat dataset curation as an AI challenge; preserve ambiguity.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the “4× less data, better OOD” win is not a clean “machine vs human generation” test — the pipeline bundles three interventions (targeting the ambiguous slice, GPT-3 generation, human relabel/filter) and the paper admits it could not ablate them. The gains most plausibly come from the shift toward ambiguous, artifact-poor examples, enabled by GPT-3 and controlled by humans — and WANLI swaps human artifacts for GPT-3’s own. Don’t over-generalise “less data is better.”

Alisa Liu (1st), S. Swayamdipta, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. Findings of EMNLP 2022. ACL · arXiv · project

We’re Afraid Language Models Aren’t Modeling Ambiguity (AmbiEnt) 1st author

A 1,645-example linguist-annotated benchmark showing even GPT-4 largely fails to recognise and disentangle the multiple meanings of ambiguous sentences.

Intro — why

Ambiguity is intrinsic to language; good assistants should recognise it, not silently pick one reading. It matters for correctness and for spotting deliberately misleading language. Yet ambiguous examples are usually excluded when benchmarks are built, so the skill went unstudied.

Method — how

Define ambiguity through entailment: an ambiguous premise yields a set of NLI labels; disambiguating it collapses the set. AmbiEnt: 1,645 examples (35% ambiguous), 142 curated + the rest InstructGPT-generated, annotated by 37 linguistics students (two each + a third validator). Types: pragmatic ~45%, lexical ~20%, syntactic ~9%, scopal ~8%, etc. Three tests: generate the disambiguations, recognise them (true/false), and model interpretation-specific continuations.

Results

GPT-4’s generated disambiguations are judged correct only 32% of the time vs 89.7% human agreement with the gold ones. It is internally inconsistent: for 76% of pairs it says a sentence “may mean” both readings yet “can only mean” each one; under the strict recognition test it drops to 2.5% (below 6.25% chance). Interestingly FLAN-T5 (worst at generation) wins the continuation test at 81%. Applied to misleading political claims, a multilabel NLI model flags ambiguity at 88.8% recall (low precision, but the “false positives” were often real overlooked ambiguities).

Discussion & future

Much NLI label “disagreement” is really ambiguity (κ jumps from 0.12 to 0.67 after disambiguation). English-only; small; results swing by how you operationalise the test. Future: larger, naturally-occurring, multilingual ambiguity data.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the famous “32% vs 90%” gap is not a clean apples-to-apples accuracy comparison — 32% is human-judged correctness of GPT-4’s generated disambiguations, while ~90% is agreement with gold ones. The authors warn 32% may even overstate GPT-4 (it “shortcuts” by restating sentences), and rankings flip across the three tests. The robust takeaway is directional: ambiguity is a severe, unsolved challenge for all current LMs.

Alisa Liu (1st), Z. Wu, J. Michael, A. Suhr, P. West, A. Koller, S. Swayamdipta, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. EMNLP 2023. arXiv · data/code

Tuning Language Models by Proxy (proxy-tuning) 1st author

Make a large frozen model behave as if fine-tuned by adding, at decode time, the logit delta between a small tuned model and its small untuned twin.

Intro — why

Fine-tuning the largest models is costly, and API-only models can’t be tuned at all. Can we get the effect of fine-tuning a giant model while only ever tuning a small one?

Method — how

Three models sharing a vocabulary: large base M, small tuned M⁺, small untuned M⁻. At each step:

p(next) = softmax[ sM + ( sM+ − sM− ) ]

The delta (s_M⁺−s_M⁻) isolates what tuning changed at small scale (it cancels the shared base behaviour); adding it transplants that change onto the big model while keeping its superior knowledge. It is exactly the DExperts arithmetic, repurposed so the “experts” are a tuned/untuned pair.

Results

Proxy-tuning Llama2-70B with 7B proxies closes 88% of the gap to the truly fine-tuned chat model (13B: 91%). AlpacaFarm win-rate 3.7% → 88.0%; GSM math 9.6 → 32.0 (vs 51.8 fully tuned); ToxiGen 67.4% → 0.0%; and on open-ended TruthfulQA it is more truthful than real fine-tuning (92.3% vs 85.8%). Decode-time overhead ~1.5–2.4×.

Discussion & future

Reasoning is where the gap is least closed; preserves base knowledge (less “alignment tax”). Future: cross-vocabulary alignment; broader API logit access.

⚠ Most important thing missing: “tune a black-box / API-only model” is aspirational. The method needs the big model’s logits over the full shared vocabulary at every step and a matching tokenizer. The 88% headline is on the open-weights Llama2-70B case; most commercial APIs expose only top-k log-probs (or nothing) and may use a different tokenizer.

Alisa Liu (1st), X. Han, Y. Wang, Y. Tsvetkov, Y. Choi, N. A. Smith. COLM 2024 (spotlight). arXiv

Data Mixture Inference: What do BPE Tokenizers Reveal about their Training Data? co-first author

The ordered merge list inside a BPE tokenizer leaks the proportions of languages / code / domains in its training data — recoverable by solving a linear program.

Intro — why

The pretraining-data composition of top models is secret, yet it determines capabilities, biases, and reproducibility. This work finds a released artifact — the tokenizer — that leaks it.

Method — how

BPE training repeatedly merges the most frequent adjacent pair, producing an ordered merge list. Key insight: the pair merged at step t was the most frequent pair after the first t−1 merges — a clue about the data mixture. Solve a linear program: unknown proportions α (≥0, sum to 1); reference samples give each category’s pair counts; every merge becomes an inequality (“under α, the merged pair really was most frequent”); minimise total violation. No weights, gradients, or training data needed.

Results

On tokenizers trained from known mixtures it is extraordinarily accurate (log₁₀ MSE ≈ −7.3 for languages, ≈ −6.5 for code — 4–6 orders of magnitude better than random). On real tokenizers: GPT-2 ≈ 99% English; GPT-3.5 ≈ 63% code (the first code-heavy GPT); GPT-4o ≈ 39% non-English (a big multilingual jump from GPT-3.5’s 3.2%); Llama 3 ≈ 48% non-English; Mistral NeMo ≈ 47% non-English; Claude ≈ 57% code.

Discussion & future

Distribution shift (reference samples ≠ real data) degrades accuracy sharply; unmodeled categories hurt; English domains are harder than languages; it only works on BPE (ordered-merge) tokenizers. Future: inferring more global properties of training data.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the percentages describe the tokenizer’s training sample, not necessarily the model’s full pretraining corpus — and the paper shows this can break (Llama’s tokenizer was trained on a different distribution than its LM). Read “GPT-4o is 39% non-English” as “GPT-4o’s tokenizer data was ~39% non-English.” Trust the directional conclusions, not the exact decimals.

J. Hayase*, Alisa Liu* (co-first — the paper footnote states “equal contribution, ordered alphabetically”), Y. Choi, S. Oh, N. A. Smith. NeurIPS 2024. arXiv · code

SuperBPE: Space Travel for Language Models co-first author

A tokenizer that lets tokens cross spaces (“superwords”), learned by a two-stage BPE curriculum, so text needs far fewer tokens and the model is both better and cheaper.

Intro — why

Standard BPE first splits on whitespace (“pretokenization”), so no token can cross a space. That wastes tokens on multi-word expressions (“by the way”), is inconsistent across languages (English “spacesuit helmet” = one German word), and makes BPE add ever-rarer subwords. More tokens means more compute — and worse models.

Method — how

A simple curriculum with one knob, a transition point t. Stage 1 (first t merges): ordinary BPE with whitespace splitting — learn subwords. Stage 2 (remaining merges): disable whitespace splitting, so frequent multi-word sequences become single superword tokens. The vocabulary budget is fixed (200k); only how it’s learned changes. Learning subwords first is essential — a “no pretokenization from the start” variant does worse.

Results

At a fixed 200k vocabulary, up to 33% fewer tokens; bytes/token rise from BPE’s ~4.45 (capped at 4.68) to 6.63. At 8B parameters (model size, vocab, and compute held fixed) the best model (t=180k) gives +4.0% average across 30 tasks (wins 25/30; +8.2% MMLU) while using ~27% less inference compute. The one significant loss is LAMBADA.

Discussion & future

Why it works: segmentation is more uniform in per-token difficulty. The most-compressing tokenizer (t=80k) is not the best model — compression ≠ quality. English-only at 8B; the cross-lingual motivation isn’t experimentally tested. Future: adapt already-trained models to SuperBPE; combine with multi-token prediction.

⚠ Most important thing missing: is it a free lunch? It holds up because the comparison fixes compute, not tokens — same model size, vocab, and FLOPs, with the effective byte-context matched, so SuperBPE doesn’t cheat by seeing more context. The honest residual catch is the authors’ own: encoding efficiency and downstream quality are different axes (you can’t rank the two tokenizers by loss). Takeaway: “a better tokenizer at equal cost,” not “more compression is always better.”

Alisa Liu* & J. Hayase* (co-first), V. Hofmann, S. Oh, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. COLM 2025. arXiv · site

Your turn: discussion questions

  1. “Steer at decode time” vs fine-tuning. DExperts → proxy-tuning is one idea applied twice. What else could decode-time steering solve, and where does it break versus actually fine-tuning the weights?
  2. Does better data really beat more data? WANLI leans on AI-generated, human-curated data and can’t ablate which part helps. When does synthetic data genuinely help, and when does it just launder the generator’s biases?
  3. Why was tokenization ignored for so long? SuperBPE and Data Mixture Inference show the tokenizer has big downstream and transparency effects. Why is such a fundamental step under-studied — and what would you investigate next?
