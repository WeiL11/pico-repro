💡 What I find most interesting

Across his papers, Yen makes the same intellectual move: inject learning into systems that already work, rather than replacing them. Orca keeps classic TCP Cubic and adds an RL supervisor on top; DeepCC leaves your TCP alone and just caps its window; Sage learns from decades of existing heuristics instead of from scratch; ASMR learns which measurements to take to speed up an existing MRI pipeline. “Augment, don’t replace — and learn from what experts already built” is his signature.

Underneath is a consistent taste for sequential, real-time decisions under uncertainty from partial, expensive observations — act well now, given only what you’ve seen so far. He is fundamentally a networked-systems researcher (most of his work, and most of his citations, are RL congestion control, nearly all co-first with Soheil Abbasloo under advisor H. J. Chao); ASMR (ICML 2024) is a genuine, recent domain jump into medical imaging with a different NYU lab. (Synthesis from his homepage + the papers below.)

Orca — “Classic Meets Modern” co-first

A hybrid congestion-control scheme: classic TCP Cubic does the per-packet work while a deep-RL agent periodically scales Cubic’s congestion window — learning’s adaptivity without discarding proven TCP.

Intro — why

Congestion control decides how fast to send so you fill the network pipe (the bandwidth-delay product) without overflowing router queues; the tension is throughput (want high) vs latency/RTT (want low, hurt by queuing). Classic Cubic is loss-based: it fills big buffers (high delay) and backs off on non-congestion loss (wastes bandwidth). Pure “clean-slate” learned schemes (Aurora, Remy, PCC) have overhead and generalize badly to unseen networks. Orca’s thesis: keep a battle-tested classic scheme as the safe controller, add a learned supervisor.

Method — how

Two-level control. Fine-grained: plain Cubic runs underneath per-ACK (stability, low overhead, TCP-friendliness). Coarse-grained: every monitoring interval a deep-RL agent applies a multiplicative factor to Cubic’s window (action ≈ 2α, −2<α<2) — it scales Cubic, never sets the raw rate, which keeps behaviour inside a safe envelope. State = recent throughput/loss/delay; reward ≈ “power” (rate ÷ delay). A recurrent net trained offline (TD3/DDPG-style) in emulation; agent in user space, Cubic in kernel.

Results

Consistently strong across diverse Internet/emulated networks, greatly reducing the generalization/overhead problems of clean-slate schemes; only ~6 hours of training; overhead on par with Cubic/BBR; friendly to competing Cubic flows. Exact %-gains vs Cubic/BBR/Aurora: unverified (SIGCOMM PDF paywalled).

Discussion & future

Near-deployable (low overhead, TCP-friendly). Open: still many quasi-hand-tuned design choices; kernel↔user-space split adds overhead; emulator-vs-reality gap. Future: transfer/meta-learning to adapt to new networks.

⚠ Most important thing missing: Orca’s adaptivity is bounded by the classic scheme it sits on. Restricting the RL action to a bounded tweak of Cubic’s window is why it generalizes better than clean-slate schemes — but it’s a ceiling: Orca can correct Cubic, not discover a radically different strategy, so where Cubic’s loss-based premise is simply wrong, the gains shrink. The bet: bounded-but-reliable beats unbounded-but-fragile for the real Internet.

S. Abbasloo*, Chen-Yu Yen* (co-first, 2nd-listed; per his homepage), H. J. Chao. SIGCOMM 2020. ACM · code · evidence

DeepCC — TCP great for cellular networks co-first

A deep-RL plug-in that sits on top of any TCP and caps its congestion window to keep latency low on volatile cellular links — without replacing TCP.

Intro — why

Cellular bandwidth swings wildly at millisecond timescales, and towers keep deep buffers → bufferbloat: throughput-greedy TCPs (Cubic, BBR) fill those buffers, getting high throughput but huge self-inflicted latency — bad for gaming/VR/video calls. The bet: don’t reinvent TCP (hard to deploy); boost the TCP you already have to hit an app’s delay target.

Method — how

Treat the underlying TCP as a black box and only cap its window: cwnd_max = 2α·cwnd, α∈[−1,1]. The TCP still probes bandwidth between caps (so it beats a pure clean-slate version by ~10% utilization). RL loop per RTT: state = avg delay, #samples, delivery rate, cwnd (normalized vs Target, stacked over 20 steps); action = continuous α; reward + below Target, − above. Trained with DDPG. A lightweight kernel “Monitor” shim makes it work under Cubic/BBR/Westwood/Illinois unchanged. The RL agent here is a user-space prototype.

Results

Boosting Cubic / BBR → ~300% / ~175% lower queuing delay; Illinois → ~4× lower delay at ~9% lower throughput. Dcubic: 4× lower delay, throughput −~11%. Beats Sprout, Verus, C2TCP, Copa, LEDBAT, PCC-Vivace, Remy, Indigo, Aurora; fairness Jain 0.936–0.987; real-world ≈ emulation. (Evaluated on 23+ real LTE traces + NYC field tests.)

Discussion & future

No theoretical guarantees (true of all CC); only boosts throughput-oriented TCP to cut delay; leans on cellular per-client isolation. Overhead: user-space agent is the bottleneck → kernel implementation is future work.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the numbers come from a user-space prototype validated mostly on single-flow cellular links — the setting that hides the hard problems. (1) Running a 2×1000 DDPG net every RTT in user space is costly; line-rate, many-socket behaviour isn’t shown. (2) On a shared bottleneck, a flow voluntarily holding back to hit a delay target can be starved by an aggressive Cubic/BBR flow — the fairness numbers were measured among DeepCC flows, not a hostile mix.

S. Abbasloo, Chen-Yu Yen (co-first per homepage; 2nd-listed), H. J. Chao. IEEE JSAC 39(1), 2021. arXiv (precursor) · evidence

Sage — “Computers Can Learn from the Heuristic Designs…” co-first

A congestion-control policy a computer learned by watching decades of human-designed CC schemes (offline RL) — distilling many heuristics into one standalone agent that improves on its teachers.

Intro — why

Hand-designed heuristics (Cubic, BBR) are robust but plateau; clean-slate learned CC generalizes badly. Sage asks: instead of designing by hand or learning from zero, can a computer learn from the existing heuristic designs themselves? It’s the next step after Orca: rather than running alongside one heuristic at runtime, learn from many heuristics offline into a single data-driven policy.

Method — how

Treat every Linux CC scheme as a teacher; log their (state, action) traces; use offline RL to distill a policy that improves on all of them — not behavioural cloning (would only match the best teacher) and not online RL. The learner (from the released code) is Recurrent CRR (critic-regularized regression): a critic learns which teacher decisions were actually good and the policy amplifies those. Net: MLP→LSTM; 69-dim state; action = a multiplier (×0.25…×4) on the rate every 10 ms. Teacher pool included BBRv2, Cubic, Vegas; traces split into single-flow-tracking and fairness sets. Deployed as a kernel module + user-space inference.

Results

Sage’s own headline %-improvements: unverified (ACM/arXiv paywalled). What independent third-party re-runs found: strengths — resilient to non-congestion (random) loss where Cubic suffers; good same-RTT fairness. Weaknesses — “unstable outside its training range, oscillating its sending rate”; lowest goodput under frequent bandwidth/RTT change; weak inter-RTT fairness (no such teacher traces). (per arXiv:2510.25105 & 2510.25498)

Discussion & future

Insight: offline RL over logged heuristic behaviour can exceed the heuristics, because a critic picks the good decisions of many teachers. But you only learn what’s in the pool. Future: a richer, more diverse trace pool (the offline design makes adding teachers cheap).

⚠ Most important thing missing: Sage is only as good as the heuristics in its training pool — its “mastery” does not transfer to regimes it never saw a teacher operate in (independent re-evals show it destabilizes / underperforms Cubic under continuously changing conditions and unequal-RTT competition). Read “computers can master CC” as “distill and surpass the union of their teachers — within the envelope those teachers covered.”

Chen-Yu Yen (first-listed; co-first per homepage PDF footnote unverified), S. Abbasloo, H. J. Chao. SIGCOMM 2023. ACM · code · evidence

ASMR — Adaptive Sampling of k-Space for Rapid MRI Pathology Prediction 1st author

An RL agent decides, one measurement at a time, which MRI k-space columns to collect next — chosen to maximize disease detection rather than image quality — diagnosing with only ~8% of the data.

Intro — why

MRI is slow because it samples k-space (the frequency-domain data); more lines = longer scan. Faster scans could enable population-scale screening. The usual “accelerated MRI” reconstructs an image from undersampled k-space, but reconstruction is optimized for image similarity, not diagnosis — at low sampling rates reconstructions start missing pathologies. Prior work skipped reconstruction and detected pathology directly from sparse k-space, but with a fixed heuristic sampling pattern. ASMR makes that choice adaptive and learned.

Method — how

k-space is acquired sequentially → frame it as a Markov decision process and train a policy that picks the next column given what it has measured so far. State = acquired columns (the full k-space is never disclosed, like a real scanner). Action = next column (action space shrinks via dynamic masking). Reward = the log-likelihood a frozen classifier gives the true pathology label — rewarding measurements that improve the diagnosis, not fidelity. Trained with PPO; frequency-domain inputs via KSPACE-NET (Fourier conv → |iFFT| → ResNet-18). Two tricks (ablated): label-balanced training (data is 2–16% positive) and explicit action masking.

Results

fastMRI knee/brain/prostate, 8 pathology tasks (AUROC, 5 seeds). Headline: within 2% of a fully-sampled classifier using only 8% of k-space on 6/8 tasks (~12.5× acceleration). Beats non-adaptive learned sampling (EMRT 7/8; LOUPE/DPS 6/8) and reconstruction-RL (+2.88% knee, +7% brain AUC). Ablations confirm the two design tricks matter.

Discussion & future

Single-coil only (modern scanners are multi-coil); slice-level not volume-level; Cartesian column sampling. The authors stress it needs extensive clinical trials before use.

⚠ Most important thing missing: ASMR optimizes detection of a fixed, pre-specified pathology list, not a diagnostic image. So it can only “see” what it was trained to look for — an incidental finding outside the label set may sit in k-space regions it chose not to sample (bad for screening, whose whole point is catching the unexpected). And with reconstruction bypassed there is no human-readable image to inspect or override. Best read as fast triage for known conditions, paired with full imaging on positives — not a replacement for diagnostic MRI.

Chen-Yu Yen (first author; co-equal* with Singhal), R. Singhal, U. Sharma, R. Ranganath, S. Chopra, L. Pinto. ICML 2024 (PMLR v235). A different NYU lab (Pinto/Chopra orbit) — not his networking advisor Chao. PMLR · arXiv · project · evidence

Your turn: discussion questions

  1. Bounded vs clean-slate. Orca puts a bounded RL tweak on top of classic Cubic; clean-slate schemes let a net pick the raw rate. When is “bounded but reliable” the right engineering call, and when does it leave too much on the table?
  2. Can you learn beyond your teachers? Sage distills many heuristics but destabilizes outside their envelope. Is it possible for a learned controller to generalize beyond any heuristic it was shown — or is “union of teachers” the ceiling?
  3. Detection vs a readable image. ASMR buys ~12× MRI speed by collecting only data that confirms a known disease list, giving up the human-readable image that catches the unexpected. Is that trade acceptable for screening? Where’s the line in medicine?

What these notes don’t cover (completeness)

A deliberate scope note (v0.6 completeness check). We deep-read his four first/co-first papers because they best show his contribution; that leaves gaps a reader should know about:

  • CFR-RL (RL traffic engineering in SDN, JSAC 2020) is his most-cited co-authored work (~241) but he is a middle author, so it’s listed, not deep-read — it shows the RL-for-networks theme beyond congestion control.
  • SGLB (SIGCOMM 2025, ByteDance) is recent industry systems work (middle author) — the “networks at scale” chapter of his career, summarized only.
  • The notes lean toward congestion control (4 of his papers); a reader might over-index on that and miss that ASMR is a genuine domain jump, not a one-off.

Completeness-critic notes (folded in): the biggest omission was the unifying theme — now stated at the top (“augment, don’t replace”). Also: most of his citation weight sits in Orca and CFR-RL; the Orca→Sage arc is him correcting his own approach (online → offline RL); and his CC papers are nearly all co-first with Soheil Abbasloo — the collaboration is central, not incidental. The 2014 graphics paper is most likely a namesake (absent from his CV), marked unverified.
