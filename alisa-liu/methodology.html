How the Alisa Liu notes were made

The method behind the content page — a two-pass research workflow governed by three principles.

← Content (the reading notes)

TL;DR

The notes were produced by a two-pass agent workflow: Pass 1 maps the whole body of work with a few parallel agents; a verify gate fact-checks before any writing; then Pass 2 sends one agent per signature paper to deep-read it for Intro / Method / Results / Future. The output is a cited teaching page whose papers are foldable (folded by default, opened on click) so the depth stays one click away.

This second, deeper pass was added precisely because a first shallow version wasn’t good enough — the whole method now follows the three principles below.

The three guiding principles

Every agent and every section of the final page must satisfy these:

  1. Always explain the concept well. Depth over summary: each paper gets a clearly-separated Intro (the problem + why), Method (how the breakthrough works), Results (concrete numbers), and Discussion / future. Jargon is defined.
  2. Every statement aligns with what was actually researched, with clear citations. Read the primary source; never assert from memory; quote the numbers; flag anything that can’t be verified.
  3. Every agent asks “what is the most important thing missing here?” and answers it — which becomes a dedicated “most important thing missing” box on each paper in the final note.

The workflow (two passes + a verify gate)

Pass 1 — the broad map (3 parallel agents)

Google Scholar blocks automated fetching, so the agents cross-checked her homepage, DBLP, the ACL Anthology, and arXiv instead. They ran concurrently:

AgentTask
Aauthoritative publication list (sorted by date) with venues, coauthors, author position, links
Bundergraduate-friendly summaries of her signature papers
Cresearch arc / themes / bio / recurring collaborators

The three results cross-corroborated each other — independent agreement raises confidence.

Verify gate — fact-check before writing

Principle 2 in action: nothing is written until claims are checked against primary sources. This pass flagged three things that would otherwise have been wrong:

  • “Infini-gram” is not hers — it is by Jiacheng Liu, a different UW researcher. (An earlier from-memory draft got this wrong; the check caught it.)
  • DBLP namesake conflation — an unrelated HCI researcher also named “Alisa Liu” appears in DBLP; her papers were excluded.
  • An unsupported award claim (a “best paper” for DExperts) was dropped for lack of evidence.
This is the whole point of verifying first: the most confident-sounding facts are exactly the ones worth checking against a primary source before they reach students.

Pass 2 — deep-read each paper (one agent per paper)

The first content page was too shallow, so a second fan-out sent one agent per signature paper (DExperts, WANLI, AmbiEnt, proxy-tuning, Data Mixture Inference, SuperBPE). Each agent read the actual paper and returned a structured breakdown, with every number checked against the source:

  • Concept (one line) · Intro (problem + why) · Method (how it works, jargon defined, key formula) · Results (concrete numbers) · Discussion / future · Citation (with Alisa Liu’s author position).
  • And, per principle 3, an explicit answer to “what is the most important thing missing here?”

Those became the foldable <details> sections on the content page (folded by default, opened on click), each ending in a red “most important thing missing” box — for example, that DExperts inherits whatever biased definition of “toxicity” its classifier encodes, that proxy-tuning is not truly black-box (it needs logit access + a shared vocabulary), or that SuperBPE’s gains rest on fixing compute, not token count.

Honest limitations

  • Google Scholar was not directly fetchable — the list is built from her homepage + DBLP + ACL Anthology + arXiv, so per-paper citation counts are unverified (numbers were left off rather than guessed).
  • A few 2026 entries are preprints; titles/venues may change.
  • Deep reads were trusted from the per-paper agents’ primary-source checks; the signature papers are well confirmed, the long tail less so. A couple of figures are explicitly marked “reported / unverified” on the content page.
