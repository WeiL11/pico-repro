💡 What I find most interesting

Reading across her papers, the question Alisa Liu keeps returning to is: how do you change a language model’s behaviour without paying to retrain it? From DExperts (2021) to proxy-tuning (2024) she steers frozen models at decode time — the same idea, scaled up over years.

In parallel she keeps poking at the parts of the pipeline everyone takes for granted and asking “is this actually good?” — the data (WANLI), and then the tokenizer (Data Mixture Inference, SuperBPE), a step most people never question. So if I had to guess what she’s really interested in: overlooked, high-leverage layers of the stack — decoding-time interventions and tokenization — and where models silently fail (ambiguity, hallucination). The recurring move is to find a small, cheap lever that quietly moves a big model, rather than throwing more compute at it.

The research arc

Her work moves steadily toward more fundamental questions — each stage asking “what are we taking for granted in how language models are built?”

2019–2020 · before the NLP PhD

Audio / music machine learning (Northwestern)

Undergrad research on audio source separation and music generation (a DCASE 2020 Best Student Paper), plus an early commonsense-QA dataset (CODAH). The data-and-evaluation instincts show up early.

2021 · entry point

Decoding-time control

DExperts (ACL 2021): steer a frozen language model at decoding time with small “expert / anti-expert” models — control behaviour without retraining. This idea recurs for years.

2022 · fixing the data

Human–AI data creation & data quality

WANLI (EMNLP Findings 2022): pair GPT-3 generation with human editing to build harder, cleaner datasets — smart curation beats raw scale. Also Generated Knowledge Prompting.

2023 · what models get wrong

Ambiguity, failures & instruction data

The AmbiEnt ambiguity benchmark (EMNLP 2023), the widely-cited Self-Instruct (ACL 2023), translation-ambiguity, hallucination and inverse-scaling work — characterising systematic LM failures.

2024 · adaptation matures + the pivot

Inference-time adaptation → tokenizer forensics

Proxy-Tuning (COLM 2024, spotlight) generalises DExperts to cheaply adapt huge / black-box models. Data Mixture Inference (NeurIPS 2024) reverse-engineers a BPE tokenizer to reveal its training-data mix — the bridge from her data theme into tokenization.

2025–2026 · the new core

Tokenization as a high-leverage layer

SuperBPE (COLM 2025) lets tokens cross whitespace for big efficiency gains; Broken Tokens (NeurIPS 2025 spotlight), byte-level sampling, and compute-optimal tokenization follow. Parallel thread: contributing to open models (Tülu 3, OLMo, Nemotron).

The through-line in one sentence: control model behaviour (2021) → fix the data that shapes it (2022–23) → adapt models at inference + analyse their failures (2023–24) → re-examine tokenization, the humble first step, as a fundamental lever (2024–26).

DExperts: Decoding-Time Controlled Text Generation with Experts & Anti-Experts 1st author

Steer a frozen LM toward/away from an attribute by adding a small expert−anti-expert logit difference at decode time — no retraining.

Intro — why

Pretrained LMs emit toxic / off-attribute text, which blocks safe deployment. Retraining 175B+ models on filtered data is infeasible, and the strongest models are API-only. The goal: a lightweight decoding-time control that needs only the model’s output probabilities, and can steer both toward a good attribute and away from a bad one (training methods like CTRL can only steer toward).

Method — how

Three models: the frozen base M, a small expert M⁺ (fine-tuned on text with the wanted attribute) and a small anti-expert M⁻ (fine-tuned on the unwanted attribute). At each step, combine their logits (a product of experts — multiplying distributions acts like a logical AND of vetoes):

softmax( z + α (z+ − z−) )

α is a single strength knob. They first truncate to the base model’s top-k / top-p tokens, then apply the experts, so the experts can’t resurrect low-quality tail tokens (preserving fluency). An “anti-only” variant reuses the base as the expert, needing only negative examples.

Results

Detoxification (RealToxicityPrompts, Perspective API): toxicity probability drops 0.520 → ~0.12 (~4×), fluency near GPT-2, beating PPLM/DAPT/GeDi. Strikingly data-efficient: ~650 toxic comments and ~3 min of fine-tuning already match the strongest baseline. It even steers GPT-3 via the API (top-100 logprobs) to ~0.11 toxicity probability. Also controls sentiment.

Discussion & future

Negative steering is easier than positive; the anti-only variant is weak adversarially; narrow experts (movie-review sentiment) trade fluency for control. Future: personalised anti-experts and better definitions of harmful language.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the control is only as good as the attribute label. “Toxicity” is a contested construct — Perspective API over-flags minority-identity language — so DExperts faithfully steers toward whatever the data and classifier encode, biases included. Audit the anti-expert’s data and the evaluator before trusting it; the algorithm is attribute-agnostic, which is exactly why responsibility shifts onto the labels.

Alisa Liu (1st), M. Sap, X. Lu, S. Swayamdipta, C. Bhagavatula, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. ACL 2021. arXiv · ACL · code

WANLI: Worker & AI Collaboration for NLI Dataset Creation 1st author

A 108k NLI dataset built by a human–AI loop: find hard reasoning patterns, let GPT-3 draft new examples in that style, filter, then have humans revise + label.

Intro — why

NLI (Natural Language Inference): given a premise and a hypothesis, decide entailment / contradiction / neutral. Crowdsourced NLI data is full of annotation artifacts (e.g. the word “not” signalling contradiction), so models learn shortcuts and are brittle out-of-distribution. WANLI flips the roles: let the LM do the generating (its strength) and humans do the evaluating/correcting (theirs).

Method — how

Four stages. (1) Dataset cartography — track a model’s predictions across training epochs to find ambiguous examples (high-variability), which build robustness; seed from the top-25% most ambiguous MNLI. (2) For each seed, take its 4 nearest same-label neighbours (via the model’s [CLS] embedding) to capture a reasoning pattern, and ask GPT-3 to write a new pair in that pattern. (3) Automatically filter with an “estimated max variability” metric, keeping the examples that look ambiguous to the model. (4) Human review — two workers per example assign the gold label, make minimal edits, or discard (heavy revision re-introduces artifacts).

Results

107,885 labelled examples (~4× smaller than MultiNLI’s 393k). Replacing MNLI with WANLI improves a RoBERTa model on 8 out-of-distribution test sets: +4% Diagnostics, +11% HANS, +9% Adversarial NLI — and it beats baselines using up to 9× more data. A hypothesis-only model gets only 41.6% on WANLI vs 49.6% on MNLI — evidence of fewer artifacts.

Discussion & future

English-NLI only; no per-component ablations (each would need a full annotated dataset); genuinely ambiguous examples are forced into one label. Future: other languages/tasks, and preserving ambiguity rather than discarding it.

⚠ Most important thing missing: WANLI is not artifact-free — it swaps human shortcuts for GPT-3’s own (the paper finds WANLI has its own detectable word correlations). The human-in-the-loop (light revision + relabel + discard) is what keeps GPT-3’s artifacts and biases in check, which is why the authors warn against fully automating the pipeline. Don’t conclude “AI-generated data is unbiased.”

Alisa Liu (1st), S. Swayamdipta, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. Findings of EMNLP 2022. ACL · arXiv · project

We’re Afraid Language Models Aren’t Modeling Ambiguity (AmbiEnt) 1st author

A linguist-annotated benchmark (1,645 examples) showing even GPT-4 mostly fails to recognise and tease apart the multiple meanings of an ambiguous sentence.

Intro — why

Ambiguity is intrinsic to language — one sentence can carry several valid readings. The worry: LMs used as assistants tend to silently commit to one reading instead of recognising ambiguity. That causes confidently wrong answers, and lets ambiguity be weaponised (claims technically true only under one reading).

Method — how

The clever move: define ambiguity through entailment. An ambiguous premise yields a set of valid NLI labels (e.g. {entail, neutral}); disambiguating it collapses the set to one label — so ambiguity becomes measurable. AmbiEnt: 1,645 examples (~35% ambiguous), 142 curated + ~1,503 InstructGPT-generated, then annotated by 37 linguistics students. Ambiguity types: pragmatic ~45%, lexical ~20%, syntactic ~9%, scopal ~8%, etc. Three tests: generate the disambiguations, recognise them (true/false), and model interpretation-specific continuations.

Results

GPT-4’s generated disambiguations are judged correct only 32% of the time vs ~90% for the dataset’s own. Worse, it is internally inconsistent: for 76% of pairs it says a sentence “may mean” both readings yet also “can only mean” each one. Under the strict recognition test it drops to 2.5% (below 6.25% chance). Applied to misleading political claims, a multilabel NLI model flags ambiguity with ~88.8% recall (low precision, but the “false positives” were often real overlooked ambiguities).

Discussion & future

The benchmark’s distribution isn’t natural; it is English-only; scores swing by how you operationalise the test. Future: larger, naturally-occurring, multilingual ambiguity data.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the headline “32% vs 90%” is one task-specific number, not an “ambiguity IQ” — performance varies wildly across the three tests. The deeper finding is the inconsistency: the real open question is whether LMs have any stable, internally-consistent representation of ambiguity at all (the evidence leans toward “no”).

Alisa Liu (1st), Z. Wu, J. Michael, A. Suhr, P. West, A. Koller, S. Swayamdipta, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. EMNLP 2023. arXiv

Tuning Language Models by Proxy (proxy-tuning) 1st author

Make a large frozen model behave as if fine-tuned by adding, at decode time, the logit “delta” between a small tuned model and its small untuned twin.

Intro — why

Fine-tuning the largest models is very expensive, and API-only models can’t be fine-tuned at all. Proxy-tuning customises a big model using only its output logits — no weight updates.

Method — how

Three models: large base M, small tuned M⁺, small untuned M⁻. At each step:

p(next) = softmax[ sM + ( sM+ − sM− ) ]

The delta (s_M⁺ − s_M⁻) is exactly what fine-tuning changed in the small model (subtracting M⁻ cancels its generic behaviour); adding it transplants that change onto the big model. Token analysis shows the delta is larger on reasoning tokens than factual ones — tuning shifts style/reasoning, not knowledge. (It is the DExperts formula, repurposed: the “experts” are now a tuned/untuned pair of the same model.)

Results

Proxy-tuning Llama2-70B with 7B experts closes 88% of the gap to the truly fine-tuned chat model (13B: 91%). On TruthfulQA it reaches 92.3% vs 85.8% for real fine-tuning — beating it, by avoiding the “alignment tax” on knowledge. GSM math 9.6 → 32.0 (vs 51.8 fully tuned); toxicity 67.4% → 0.0%.

Discussion & future

Runs three forward passes per token; recovery is large but incomplete (math still trails). Upside: base weights are untouched, so knowledge is preserved. Future: cross-vocabulary alignment, domain/temporal adaptation.

⚠ Most important thing missing: it is not truly black-box. It needs read access to the big model’s next-token logits (or top-k logprobs) and a shared vocabulary between the big model and the small experts. A text-only API (no logprobs) or a mismatched tokenizer breaks it — it removes the need for the model’s weights but adds a hard requirement on its logits.

Alisa Liu (1st), X. Han, Y. Wang, Y. Tsvetkov, Y. Choi, N. A. Smith. COLM 2024 (spotlight). arXiv · code

Data Mixture Inference: What do BPE Tokenizers Reveal about their Training Data? 2nd / co-lead

The ordered merge list a BPE tokenizer learns is a fingerprint of its training data — solve a linear program to read off the language/code/domain proportions, with no data or weights.

Intro — why

The pretraining data of top LMs is secret, yet it determines capabilities, biases, and legal exposure. This work shows a released artifact — the tokenizer — leaks far more than anyone assumed.

Method — how

BPE builds a vocabulary by repeatedly merging the most frequent adjacent pair, producing an ordered list of merge rules. Key insight: the pair merged at step t was the most frequent pair at that moment — a clue about the data mixture. They set up a linear program: unknown proportions α (≥0, sum to 1) of each category; reference samples give each category’s pair counts; every merge becomes an inequality (“under α, the merged pair really was the most frequent”); minimise total violation. No weights, gradients, or training data required — just the public merge list.

Results

Scales to ~116 categories (112 languages, 37 programming languages, 5 domains). On tokenizers trained from known mixtures it recovers them 2–6 orders of magnitude better than random. On real tokenizers: GPT-2 ≈ 99% English; GPT-3.5 ≈ 63% code (the first code-heavy GPT); GPT-4o ≈ 39% non-English (a big multilingual jump); Llama 3 and Mistral NeMo similarly multilingual; Claude ≈ 57% code. Books punch above their weight.

Discussion & future

Distribution shift (reference samples not matching real data) sharply degrades accuracy; missing categories hurt; it only works on BPE (ordered-merge) tokenizers. Future: inferring more global properties of training data.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the percentages describe the tokenizer’s training sample, not necessarily the model’s full pretraining corpus (labs may train the tokenizer on a different sample). Trust the directional conclusions (“GPT-4o went multilingual”); don’t treat the exact decimals as the model’s literal data diet.

J. Hayase, Alisa Liu (2nd; co-lead per the repo, equal-contribution unverified), Y. Choi, S. Oh, N. A. Smith. NeurIPS 2024. arXiv · code

SuperBPE: Space Travel for Language Models 1st author

A tokenizer that lets tokens cross spaces (“superwords”), learned by a two-stage BPE curriculum, so text encodes in far fewer tokens and the model is both better and cheaper.

Intro — why

Standard BPE first splits text on whitespace, so no token ever crosses a space. That wastes tokens on multi-word expressions (“of course”), and disadvantages whitespace-free languages (Chinese). More tokens means more compute — and, the paper shows, measurably worse model quality.

Method — how

Keep BPE, but apply a pretokenization curriculum with one new knob, a transition point t: the first t merges are learned with whitespace splitting (ordinary subwords), and the remaining merges without it (so frequent multi-word sequences become single “superword” tokens). Vocabulary is fixed (200k), split by t (here t = 50k). Learning subwords first is what makes superwords useful; the model architecture is unchanged.

Results

~33% fewer tokens at a fixed 200k vocabulary; at 8B parameters (model size, vocab, and compute held fixed) +4.0% absolute average across 30 tasks (wins 25/30; +8.2% MMLU), while using ~27% less inference compute. Segmentation is also more uniform in per-token difficulty.

Discussion & future

Requires training two tokenizers (small one-time cost); t is a hand-set cutoff (adaptive strategies are future work); it underperforms on a few tasks. Future: better transition strategies, multilingual / code.

⚠ Most important thing missing: the experiment fixes compute, not tokens. Because each SuperBPE token packs more text, at equal compute the model effectively sees more data — and that extra coverage is part of why it wins, not a flaw in the comparison. The honest framing is “fixed compute, not fixed tokens,” plus a structurally cheaper model at inference.

Alisa Liu (1st), J. Hayase, V. Hofmann, S. Oh, N. A. Smith, Y. Choi. COLM 2025. arXiv · site

Self-Instruct: Aligning Language Models with Self-Generated Instructions co-author

ACL 2023 · Wang, Kordi, Mishra, Liu, Smith, Khashabi, Hajishirzi · arXiv

Not deep-dived here (she is a co-author, not lead), but included as one of her most-cited works and foundational to open instruction tuning: seed an LM with a few tasks, have it generate many more instruction/response examples, filter, and fine-tune on that synthetic data — a +33% jump for vanilla GPT-3 with minimal human labelling.

Two big themes (how the papers connect)

1 · Steer the model at decode time, don’t retrain it

DExperts (expert/anti-expert) → Proxy-Tuning (steer a huge model with a small tuned one) → decode-time alignment for multiple objectives, and MaRCo for text revision. One idea — adjust the output distribution at inference — reused and scaled across years.

2 · Tokenization is an under-examined, high-leverage layer

Data Mixture Inference (the tokenizer as a data fingerprint) → SuperBPE (tokens across whitespace) → Broken Tokens (robustness to non-canonical tokenization) and byte-level sampling. The thesis: the “humble first step” quietly shapes efficiency, robustness, and even what a model can learn.

A connecting thread · data quality & LM failures

WANLI and Self-Instruct (better/synthetic data) and the failure-analysis papers (ambiguity, hallucination snowballing, inverse scaling) ask what models miss — which is what motivates fixing the data and the tokenizer.

Your turn: discussion questions

No official answers — bring your reasoning to our session.

  1. “Steer at decode time” vs fine-tuning. DExperts → proxy-tuning is the same idea applied twice. What else could decode-time steering solve, and where does it break down compared with actually fine-tuning the weights?
  2. Does better data really beat more data? WANLI and Self-Instruct lean on AI-generated / AI-assisted data. When does synthetic data genuinely help, and when does it risk baking the model’s own biases back into training?
  3. Why was tokenization ignored for so long? SuperBPE and Data Mixture Inference show the tokenizer has big downstream effects. Why is such a fundamental step under-studied — and what would you investigate next?
