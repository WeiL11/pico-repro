NeurIPS 2025Neural DynamicsSynchronizationAdaptive ComputeSakana AI

📚 For our study group

CTM asks a question most deep learning papers don’t: what if we took the temporal dynamics inside individual neurons seriously? The authors give each neuron its own private weights, let them generate rich activity histories, and then use which neurons fire together over time (synchronization) as the model’s latent representation. It’s biologically inspired — and it produces some surprising emergent behaviors.

One thing to keep in mind as you read: the authors are explicit that this is not trying to beat the state of the art. The goal is to show a new kind of neural network is possible — and to invite the community to explore it.

💡 What I find most interesting

The most surprising thing is where the information lives. In a transformer, the latent representation is a vector of activations at a given instant — a snapshot. In the CTM, the representation is a relationship: the inner product of each pair of neurons’ activity over time, St = Zt · (Zt)T. Two neurons are “synchronized” if they have tended to fire at the same internal ticks. This is exactly the neuroscience binding hypothesis — that the brain encodes “this edge and this color belong to the same object” by synchronizing the neurons that represent them. The model is not told to synchronize anything; it learns to use synchrony as a tool because it turns out to be a powerful representation.

The second surprise: adaptive compute comes for free. There is no halting module, no learned stopping signal. The model just picks the tick with the best prediction and the tick with the highest certainty, averages them in the loss, and the rest follows — easy images get answered early, hard ones keep thinking.

The CTM is a neural network architecture that explicitly incorporates neural dynamics over time as its core mechanism, rather than treating neurons as simple weighted-sum units. It is inspired by biological principles — specifically temporal coding (information encoded in the timing of neural activity) and neural synchrony (the binding hypothesis: neurons representing related features synchronize their activity). The CTM is not a language model or a vision model specifically — it is a general architecture demonstrated on diverse tasks.

The research arc

1980s–1997

RNNs and LSTMs: temporal processing, uniform neurons

Recurrent networks give neural networks a sense of time: the hidden state carries information across sequence steps. LSTMs (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) add gating for long-range memory. But all neurons share the same weight matrices — each neuron is just a row of a shared W. No individual temporal identity.

2017

Transformers: attention replaces recurrence

Vaswani et al. replace sequential processing with self-attention: all positions interact in parallel. Dominant today. But attention attends over input positions, not over an internal thought timeline. There is no time dimension decoupled from the data.

2021

PonderNet and adaptive compute: variable depth

Graves (2016) and Banino et al. (PonderNet, 2021) add an explicit halting module that lets the model stop early for easy inputs. Adaptive depth as a design choice. But it requires a dedicated halting component bolted on top of the base model.

2024–25

Renewed interest in recurrence and reasoning

Papers like Mamba and RWKV show recurrent models can compete with transformers on language. Separately, scaling laws appear to plateau, and the community starts asking what’s missing. Sakana AI argues one missing piece is neural temporal dynamics — not just recurrence, but genuine per-neuron temporal identity and synchrony.

2025

CTM: neuron-level models + synchronization as representation

Darlow, Regan, Risi, Seely, Jones (Sakana AI) give every neuron its own private weights, let them process their own activity histories, and use the inner product of those histories as the latent state. Adaptive compute emerges without a halting module. Demonstrated on mazes, ImageNet, parity, RL. NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight.

Continuous Thought Machines NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight

Give each neuron its own private temporal processing weights; use the cross-correlation of neuron activity histories (synchronization) as the latent representation; get adaptive compute for free.
🔑 Key finding. Using neural synchronization — which neurons fire together over an internal thought timeline — as the latent representation produces a model that builds internal world maps (solves mazes without positional encoding), learns to ‘look around’ images without being told to, and acquires adaptive compute depth as an emergent property of its loss function — no halting module needed.

Intro — why

Modern neural networks treat neurons as nearly identical: each neuron is a row of a shared weight matrix, receiving inputs and producing a scalar output. This is computationally convenient but biologically unrealistic — in the brain, individual neurons have unique temporal dynamics, and the timing of their firing encodes information. The binding hypothesis in neuroscience suggests that the brain links disparate features (color, shape, location) into a unified percept by synchronizing the neurons that represent them. CTM asks: what if an artificial neural network used the same principle? Instead of a single shared hidden state, give each neuron its own private MLP parameters to process its own activity history. Use the synchrony pattern of those histories as the latent representation. Run the network for T “internal ticks” that are decoupled from any input sequence — the model thinks on a self-generated timeline even for a static image.

Method — how

Setup. Standard networks: one forward pass, one hidden state, all neurons share weights. CTM: run T internal ticks on a separate “thought timeline.” At each tick t → t+1: (1) Synapse model (shared, U-NET MLP) takes the current post-activations + attention output → produces pre-activations:

at = fθsyn(concat(zt, ot)) ∈ ℝD
(2) History: store the last M pre-activations: At = [at−M+1, ..., at] ∈ ℝD×M. (3) Neuron-Level Models (NLMs): each neuron d has its OWN private parameters θd (a small MLP):
zt+1d = gθd( Atd ) (each neuron processes only its own history)
(4) Synchronization — the latent representation. Collect all post-activation histories Zt = [z1,...,zt] ∈ ℝD×t and compute:
St = Zt · (Zt)T ∈ ℝD×D
Stij is large when neurons i and j have been firing together. With learnable temporal decay rij:
Stij = (Zti · diag(Rtij) · Ztj) / √(Στ [Rtij]τ)
Sub-sample Dout neuron pairs from St, project: yt = Wout · Stout (outputs), qt = Win · Staction (attention query). (5) Cross-attention to data:
ot = Attention(Q = qt,  KV = FeatureExtractor(data)) (attention uses synchrony as the query)
Adaptive compute (no halting module). The model outputs yt at every tick. For each data point, pick dynamically: t1 = argmint(loss), t2 = argmaxt(certainty):
L = ( Lt1 + Lt2 ) / 2
Since t1 and t2 are data-dependent, easy inputs get answered at small t1 — fewer ticks used, no explicit stop signal needed.

Results

2D Mazes (39×39, no positional encoding): CTM significantly outperforms LSTM (1–3 layers) and feed-forward baselines across all path lengths. Trained on 39×39, generalizes to 99×99 mazes by re-applying the same learned policy — suggesting it learned a general procedure, not a lookup. Emergent: keeps exploring beyond its training horizon. ImageNet-1K (50 internal ticks, ResNet-152 backbone): 72.47% top-1, 89.89% top-5 (not SOTA, by design). Native adaptive compute: at 0.8 certainty threshold, most examples decided in <10 of 50 ticks. Excellent calibration emerges naturally. Learns to ‘look around’ images (Figure 2b): attention traces intuitive inspection paths without any training signal for this behavior. Parity (64-bit sequences): CTM with 75–100 ticks achieves perfect accuracy on some seeds; LSTM plateaus at ~60%. Attention head develops interpretable left-to-right scanning strategy (Figure 6d).

Discussion & future

The authors are explicit this is not a benchmark paper. The contribution is: showing the architecture is possible and surprisingly capable, and inviting the community to build on it. The emergent adaptive compute and interpretable attention are the most interesting findings — behaviors that arise without being explicitly trained for. Future work (from the paper): scaling NLMs, applying to language models, understanding WHY synchrony works as a representation, and exploring the formal connection to spiking neural networks.

⚠ Most important thing missing: This is explicitly not a SOTA paper. ImageNet 72.47% top-1 is far below modern models (~90%+). The O(D²) synchronization matrix grows expensive at large D, and each neuron having private weights increases parameter count. There are no language model experiments — the most obvious next direction. The theoretical reason WHY synchrony is a good representation remains open. Seed sensitivity in the parity task suggests some instability. The comparison is against LSTM and FF baselines; no comparison with modern SSMs (Mamba, RWKV), modern transformers, or PonderNet on the same tasks.

Your turn: discussion questions

  1. The CTM uses synchronization St = Zt · (Zt)T as its latent, rather than a single snapshot zt. The paper says 'snapshot representations were too constraining.' Can you think of a task where the snapshot would be equally good? And one where synchrony gives a meaningful advantage?
  2. The CTM achieves adaptive compute without a halting module: it just picks the tick of minimum loss and the tick of maximum certainty per sample, and averages their losses. Does this feel like 'true' adaptive compute, or is it more like a soft attention over ticks? How would you know which ticks the model actually relies on?
  3. The maze experiment uses no positional encodings, forcing the CTM to build an internal world model. It then generalizes from 39×39 to 99×99 by re-applying the same policy. Is this convincing evidence of a 'general procedure'? What experiment would you design to further test whether the CTM has actually learned something like graph search?
  4. Neuron-Level Models give each neuron its own private MLP of width dhidden. The total parameter count scales as D × dhidden² for the NLMs alone. For D=1024 and dhidden=64, that's 4M parameters just for NLMs. How do you think the benefits and costs of NLMs would scale as D grows to 10k or 100k?
  5. The paper positions CTM as distinct from Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs): it uses continuous values and is trained with backprop, while SNNs use discrete spike events and often require specialized learning rules. But the inspiration (neural timing, synchrony) is similar. What would a hybrid look like? What would you lose and gain?

What these notes don’t cover (completeness)

These notes cover the CTM paper and its immediate context. Not covered:

  • Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs): discrete spike timing, event-driven computation, hardware efficiency — the CTM draws inspiration from but is explicitly distinct.
  • Liquid State Machines & Reservoir Computing: random fixed recurrent networks with only the readout trained; related in spirit to rich internal dynamics.
  • Mamba & modern SSMs: selective state space models; strong on language; not compared against CTM in the paper.
  • Neuroscience binding hypothesis: the theoretical basis for synchrony as a binding mechanism (Gray & Singer 1989, Eckhorn et al. 1988); the paper motivates CTM with this but doesn’t deeply engage with the neuroscience literature.
  • Language modeling: CTM is not tested on any language task; this is the most obvious next direction and explicitly deferred.
