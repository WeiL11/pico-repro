This page covers speculative decoding — the technique behind DeepSeek’s DeepSpec open-source framework. The big question: how do you serve an LLM 60–85% faster without changing the model or its output? The answer turns out to involve a clever trick about what transformers can do in parallel, and a surprising discovery that a tiny sequential correction on top of a parallel drafter is worth far more than making the drafter bigger.

Reading order: Leviathan 2023 (the idea) → EAGLE-3 (autoregressive frontier) → DFlash (break the sequential wall) → DSpark (fix what breaks & ship to production).

💡 What I find most interesting

The most interesting thing here is a counterintuitive empirical result: fully parallel draft models (DFlash) outperform fully sequential ones (EAGLE-3) on average accepted length — even though each parallel position ignores all the others. The reason is that the first draft position carries the highest leverage (a miss there throws away the entire block), and a parallel model can afford a much deeper architecture because its drafting time doesn’t grow with block size. DSpark’s insight: get the depth advantage at position 1 and fix the dependency problem at positions 2–7 with a tiny sequential head that costs almost nothing. “A little autoregression goes a long way.”

DeepSpec is an algorithm-driven training repository for speculative decoding draft models, open-sourced by DeepSeek-AI alongside the DeepSpark paper. It contains data preparation, training code, and evaluation scripts for three draft-model algorithms. The headline algorithm, DSpark, is currently deployed inside DeepSeek-V4 (Flash and Pro) serving real user traffic.

The research arc

2022–23

Speculative decoding invented

Leviathan, Kalman & Matias (Google) show that a target transformer can verify γ candidate tokens in one forward pass — the same cost as generating 1. Use a cheap draft model to propose the candidates, run rejection sampling, and generation is 2–3× faster with identical output distribution. Accepted at ICML 2023 as an Oral. DeepMind’s Chen et al. independently reach the same idea (“speculative sampling”).

2024

EAGLE: smarter autoregressive drafting

Li et al. propose drafting at the feature level (predicting next hidden states, not next tokens), which gives tighter coupling to the target model. EAGLE-2 adds dynamic draft tree construction. Strong baseline, but autoregressive: T_draft ∝ γ.

2025

EAGLE-3: training-time test, scales with data

The feature-prediction approach hits a ceiling: more training data doesn’t help because the feature distribution at inference differs from training. EAGLE-3 switches to direct token prediction, adds multi-layer feature fusion, and introduces training-time test (TTT) to close the train/test gap. Reaches ~6.5× speedup. Still autoregressive.

2026 (Jan)

DFlash: the sequential wall falls

Chen, Liang & Liu (ICML 2026) inject the target model’s hidden states into the draft model as KV context, then let all γ draft positions attend bidirectionally to each other and to the target context. Result: all γ tokens in a single forward pass. T_draft ≈ O(1) w.r.t. γ. Gains 2.5× over EAGLE-3. But without inter-token dependency, later positions suffer “suffix decay.”

2026 (Jun)

DSpark: fix what breaks, ship to production

DeepSeek-AI + Peking University release DSpark: (1) a semi-autoregressive head that adds intra-block dependency at near-zero latency cost on top of DFlash’s parallel backbone, and (2) a confidence-scheduled verifier that dynamically truncates low-confidence draft suffixes based on real-time engine load. Deployed in DeepSeek-V4. Open-sourced as DeepSpec. 60–85% faster than MTP-1 baseline at matched throughput.

Fast Inference from Transformers via Speculative Decoding foundational

A cheap draft model proposes a block of tokens; the target model verifies all in one parallel pass and accepts the longest consistent prefix. Identical output, 2–3× faster.
🔑 Key finding. Transformer verification is free in parallel: verifying γ tokens costs the same as generating 1, so any cheap draft model that proposes plausible tokens yields a lossless speedup. On T5-XXL: 2–3× faster with mathematically identical outputs.

Intro — why

Autoregressive decoding is a serial bottleneck: each token requires a full forward pass. The insight is that verification is much cheaper than generation: a target transformer can score all γ positions in parallel in one pass (the same cost as producing 1 token) by leveraging the fact that attention is already computed over all positions. So if a small, fast draft model can propose γ plausible candidates, we get γ tokens for the price of 1 verification.

Method — how

Setup. You have a large target model Mt and a much cheaper draft model Md. Baseline (standard decoding):

L = Ttarget × (number of tokens) (one full forward pass per token)
What speculative decoding changes. Draft Md proposes a block x1,...,xγ. Mt runs one forward pass, computing pt(xk) for all k simultaneously. Accept each token:
accept xk with prob min(1, pt(xk) / pd(xk)) (rejection sampling)
First rejection at k discards xk,...,xγ; Mt always generates one bonus token. Why the output is identical: the accept/reject rule is exactly the Radon-Nikodym correction that recovers pt’s distribution even when the draft is wrong. Latency per accepted token: L = (Tdraft + Tverify) / τ, where τ = expected accepted tokens/cycle. Improving τ (better draft) or lowering Tdraft (faster draft) both help.

Results

T5-XXL vs T5X baseline: 2–3× speedup. Output distribution is mathematically identical (proven via rejection sampling theory). No model retraining. Works for any pair of models (no architectural constraint); practical speedup depends on the draft acceptance rate and the draft model’s cost.

Discussion & future

The entire subsequent literature (EAGLE, DFlash, DSpark) is about answering: how do you maximize τ at minimum Tdraft? Tree-based verification (Miao et al. 2024) extends the idea by verifying multiple draft paths in one target pass.

⚠ Most important thing missing: Speedup is limited by the draft acceptance rate τ, which depends entirely on draft quality. If the draft is bad (low τ), the verification overhead dominates and you can slow down vs greedy decoding. The paper doesn’t solve the draft-quality problem — that’s what EAGLE, DFlash, and DSpark are about.

Yaniv Leviathan, Matan Kalman, Yossi Matias (Google). Fast Inference from Transformers via Speculative Decoding. ICML 2023 (Oral). arXiv:2211.17192 · evidence

EAGLE-3: Scaling up Inference Acceleration via Training-Time Test autoregressive frontier

Direct token prediction + multi-layer feature fusion + a training-time test technique that closes the train/test distribution gap — lets the draft model scale with more data, reaching ~6.5× speedup.
🔑 Key finding. Switching from feature prediction to token prediction, fusing hidden states from multiple target layers, and bridging the train/test gap with training-time test (TTT) lets the draft model finally scale with more data: up to 6.5× speedup and ~1.4× over EAGLE-2.

Intro — why

EAGLE (2024) drafts by predicting the target’s next hidden state (feature-level autoregression). Problem: the hidden-state distribution at inference differs from training (the model hasn’t seen the specific prompt’s features during training). More data hits diminishing returns. EAGLE-3 rethinks the prediction target: draft directly in token space, use multi-layer features, and make training look like inference.

Method — how

Setup. An autoregressive draft model conditions each position on all previously sampled tokens, giving high quality at later positions — but Tdraft ∝ γ, forcing small block sizes. Three changes in EAGLE-3: (1) Token prediction: the draft model predicts the next token id directly, not the next hidden state:

p(xk+1 | x≤k, Hmulti) (token-space, not feature-space)
(2) Multi-layer fusion: feed hidden states from multiple target layers Hmulti = [H(l1);...;H(lm)] so the draft sees richer representations. (3) Training-Time Test (TTT): during training, augment the input features to simulate the inference-time distribution — specifically, at TTT horizon T=7, the draft model is also conditioned on its own previous outputs from earlier timesteps (as it would be at test time). This closes the train/test gap and allows more training data to actually improve accuracy.

Results

Up to 6.5× speedup on diverse benchmarks, ~1.4× over EAGLE-2. In the DeepSpec comparison (Table 1, same setup as DFlash/DSpark): Qwen3-4B GSM8K τ = 5.14, MATH500 = 4.62 — strong on structured tasks, weaker on chat (MT-Bench = 2.39, Alpaca = 2.26). Still slower than DFlash/DSpark because Tdraft ∝ γ.

Discussion & future

EAGLE-3 represents the practical ceiling for autoregressive drafters. The TTT insight (making training look like inference) is reusable beyond EAGLE. Whether the token-vs-feature question matters more than architecture depth remains open.

⚠ Most important thing missing: EAGLE-3 is still autoregressive: Tdraft grows linearly with block size γ, forcing block sizes around 4–7 to keep drafting latency low. DFlash and DSpark can afford γ=16 or more at the same latency budget. This is the fundamental gap, not a deficit in TTT or multi-layer fusion.

Yuhui Li, Fangyun Wei, Chao Zhang, Hongyang Zhang. EAGLE-3: Scaling up Inference Acceleration of Large Language Models via Training-Time Test. arXiv 2025. arXiv:2503.01840 · evidence

DFlash: Block Diffusion for Flash Speculative Decoding ICML 2026

Inject the target model's hidden states into the draft model as KV context, then let all γ draft positions attend bidirectionally in one forward pass — O(1) drafting time regardless of block size.
🔑 Key finding. By conditioning the draft on the target’s own hidden states (KV injection) and generating all tokens via a single bidirectional forward pass, DFlash achieves >6× lossless speedup and 2.5× over EAGLE-3 — but acceptance decays at later block positions because no token depends on the ones before it.

Intro — why

The sequential wall in autoregressive drafting: Tdraft ∝ γ, so you can’t afford large blocks. DFlash breaks this by making the draft model a block diffusion model: all positions are predicted simultaneously in one pass. The key enabling idea is KV injection — rich context features extracted from the target model are injected into the draft model’s key-value pairs, so the draft knows what the target would have attended to, even without conditioning on previously sampled tokens.

Method — how

Setup. Target model runs prefill, producing hidden states H(l1),...,H(lm) at layers l1,...,lm. Baseline (autoregressive drafter):

xk = draft(x1,...,xk-1)   →   Tdraft ∝ γ
What DFlash changes. Compress the target’s hidden states into a context summary, inject into draft’s attention as additional K, V:
Hctx = RMSNorm( Wc [H(l1); … ;H(lm)] )
Ki = [WiK Hctx ; WiK Hd],   Vi = [WiV Hctx ; WiV Hd]
Feed anchor token + γ mask tokens; all draft positions attend bidirectionally to each other and to Hctx. Single forward pass → all γ logits simultaneously. Tdraft ≈ O(1) w.r.t. γ. Why KV injection is the enabling trick: without it, each position has no information about what the target actually computed — the context summary Hctx gives the draft a strong prior so it can predict well even without seeing other draft tokens.

Results

>6× lossless acceleration across diverse models/tasks. Up to 2.5× over EAGLE-3. DeepSpec Table 1: Qwen3-4B GSM8K τ = 5.40 (vs Eagle3 5.14), Arena-Hard τ = 2.83 (vs Eagle3 2.55). DFlash consistently beats EAGLE-3 despite per-position independence, because the first position has much higher capacity (deeper network, O(1) cost).

Discussion & future

The suffix-decay limitation (Fig 2 in DSpark): DFlash’s conditional acceptance rate drops from 0.88 to 0.78 by position 7 on Code, and 0.72 to 0.63 on Chat, because later positions marginalize over all possible preceding tokens instead of conditioning on the one actually sampled. This motivates DSpark’s sequential head.

⚠ Most important thing missing: Suffix decay is the key unresolved problem: because positions are independent, each marginalizes over all possible preceding tokens rather than the one actually sampled. Multi-modal collisions (“of course” vs “no course”) push later positions toward contradictory predictions. DSpark’s contribution is a fix for exactly this. Also: DFlash requires its KV-injection setup to be compatible with the target model’s layer structure — cross-architecture transfer is not addressed.

Jian Chen, Yesheng Liang, Zhijian Liu. DFlash: Block Diffusion for Flash Speculative Decoding. ICML 2026. arXiv:2602.06036 · evidence

DSpark: Confidence-Scheduled Speculative Decoding with Semi-Autoregressive Generation DeepSeek 2026

Combine DFlash's fast parallel backbone with a lightweight sequential head that injects intra-block dependency — then add a confidence-based verifier that dynamically prunes the draft block to maximize system throughput. Deployed in DeepSeek-V4.
🔑 Key finding. A tiny sequential correction on top of DFlash’s parallel backbone raises macro-average accepted length by +16–18% vs DFlash and +27–31% vs Eagle3, while a confidence scheduler routes verification compute only to tokens likely to survive — together achieving 60–85% faster per-user generation in DeepSeek-V4 production.

Intro — why

DFlash showed that parallel drafting beats autoregressive drafting on average. But Figure 2 in the DSpark paper reveals a clean mechanistic story: DFlash wins at position 1 (deeper architecture, ~0.88 vs Eagle3’s 0.81 on Math) but loses by positions 2–7 (acceptance decays to 0.78 on Code, while Eagle3 is stable or rising). DSpark’s design question: can we get DFlash’s capacity advantage at position 1 plus Eagle3’s dependency quality at later positions, without making the drafter slow? Answer: yes — the sequential correction is so cheap it barely changes Tdraft.

Method — how

Setup. Starting from DFlash as the parallel backbone: one forward pass produces hidden states h1,...,hγ and base logits U1,...,Uγ. The base logits treat each position independently (no dependency). Component 1: Semi-autoregressive generation. Add a lightweight sequential head that computes a prefix-dependent transition bias Bk(x0, x<k, xk), blending it with the parallel logits:

pk(v | x0, x<k) = softmax( Uk(v) + Bk(x0, x<k, v) )
The Markov head variant: B(xk−1, ·) = W1[xk−1]·W2 (low-rank r=256 embedding lookup + projection). Just looking up one token and adding a bias — negligible latency. The RNN head variant keeps a recurrent state sk for full history:
sk = σ(Wgzk) ⊙ sk-1 + (1−σ(Wgzk)) ⊙ tanh(Wczk)
A 2-layer DSpark outperforms a 5-layer DFlash (Figure 3): sequential modeling is more parameter-efficient than parallel depth. Component 2: Confidence-scheduled verification. A confidence head predicts P(token k survives | prefix accepted):
ck = σ( w⊤ [hk ; W1[xk-1]] )
Prefix survival probability: ar,j = ∏i≤j cr,i. The hardware-aware prefix scheduler then picks per-request verification lengths ℓ1,...,ℓR to maximize system throughput:
maximize Θ = τ × SPS(B)    where   τ = Σr(1 + Σj≤ℓr ar,j),   B = Σr(1 + ℓr)
SPS(B) is the engine’s profiled throughput curve (steps/sec vs batch size). Greedy solution: sort all (request, position) candidates by survival probability ar,j descending; admit until Θ stops improving. Sequential Temperature Scaling calibrates ck position-by-position so the cumulative product accurately reflects true acceptance probability.

Results

Offline (Table 1, no confidence scheduler): vs DFlash on Qwen3-4B — GSM8K: 6.11 vs 5.40; MBPP: 5.13 vs 4.40; MT-Bench: 3.64 vs 3.07. Macro-avg improvement: +16.3% (4B), +18.4% (8B), +18.3% (14B) over DFlash; +30.9%, +26.7%, +30.0% over Eagle3. Production (DeepSeek-V4 live traffic): vs MTP-1 baseline: 60–85% faster per-user generation at matched throughput. Enables SLA tiers (120 TPS Flash, 50 TPS Pro) that were previously unattainable. Shifts the Pareto frontier of LLM serving.

Discussion & future

The production results are the most important: DSpark doesn’t just improve a benchmark metric, it shifts the feasibility frontier — performance tiers that were impossible before become achievable. The confidence scheduler is why: under high concurrency, verifying low-confidence suffix tokens wastes batch capacity that could serve other requests; adaptive truncation recovers that capacity. All evaluation uses non-thinking mode; thinking mode (chain-of-thought) deferred.

⚠ Most important thing missing: Production results are compelling but not controlled: the comparison is vs the prior MTP-1 baseline, not vs DFlash or Eagle3 under the same serving setup. Also: all results are in non-thinking mode; thinking-mode (chain-of-thought) performance is explicitly deferred. The scheduler’s greedy optimality relies on the SPS(B) curve being unimodal (smoothly decreasing); non-smooth hardware profiles and asynchronous pipelines require additional engineering (Appendix A).

Your turn: discussion questions

  1. The DSpark paper shows a 2-layer DSpark outperforms a 5-layer DFlash. What does this tell us about the relative value of depth (more parameters) vs inter-token dependency modeling? Is there a general lesson here for architecture design?
  2. The confidence scheduler maximizes Ψ = τ × SPS(B) subject to a non-anticipating constraint. Why does losslessness require the non-anticipating property? What would go wrong if the scheduler could look at all draft tokens before deciding verification lengths?
  3. DFlash generates all γ tokens bidirectionally in one pass. EAGLE-3 generates them autoregressively left-to-right. For which types of tasks do you expect each approach to have a larger advantage — and why? (Hint: think about whether the task’s structure makes later tokens predictable from earlier ones.)
  4. DSpark is deployed in DeepSeek-V4 serving real user traffic but evaluated only in non-thinking mode. What challenges do you anticipate for applying confidence-scheduled verification to chain-of-thought (thinking mode) outputs, where early rejection of a reasoning step could have outsized downstream consequences?
  5. The hardware-aware scheduler uses a pre-profiled SPS(B) curve. How should the scheduler be adapted if the serving system has heterogeneous hardware (different GPU types with different SPS curves) across requests in the same batch?

What these notes don’t cover (completeness)

These notes cover the draft-model design axis of speculative decoding. Not covered:

  • Tree-based verification (SpecInfer, Miao et al. 2024): verifying multiple draft paths in one target pass — orthogonal to draft quality and composable with DSpark.
  • Draft model training data: DeepSpec uses Open-PerfectBlend in non-thinking mode; the effect of thinking-mode training data is deferred in the paper.
  • Cross-architecture portability: DSpark/DFlash inherit KV-injection coupling to the target’s layer structure. How to transfer across architectures is not addressed.
  • Draft model compression: quantizing the draft model to reduce T_draft further.
  • Medusa and variants: parallel draft heads on top of the target model itself (no separate draft model).
